1. Solution

    First we must calculate the posterior probabilities, Pr(θ1∣x) and Pr(θ2∣x) for x=x1,x2,x3. Since θ1+θ2=1, θ1 and θ2 form a partition, and so we can use Bayes Theorem as follows. We have:

    Pr(θ1∣x1)=Pr(X=x1∣θ1)Pr(θ1)Pr(X=x1∣θ1)Pr(θ1)+Pr(X=x1∣θ2)Pr(θ2)=0.94*0.40.94*0.4+0.28*0.6=0.6912,to 4 d.p.

    Pr(θ1∣x2)=Pr(X=x2∣θ1)Pr(θ1)Pr(X=x2∣θ1)Pr(θ1)+Pr(X=x2∣θ2)Pr(θ2)=0.04*0.40.04*0.4+0.43*0.6=0.0584,to 4 d.p.

    Pr(θ1∣x3)=Pr(X=x3∣θ1)Pr(θ1)Pr(X=x3∣θ1)Pr(θ1)+Pr(X=x3∣θ2)Pr(θ2)=0.02*0.40.02*0.4+0.29*0.6=0.044,to 4 d.p.

    It follows that:

    Pr(θ2∣x1)=1−Pr(θ1∣x1)=0.3088 Pr(θ2∣x2)=1−Pr(θ1∣x2)=0.9416
    Pr(θ2∣x3)=1−Pr(θ1∣x3)=0.956

    And so we obtain the posterior distributions Pr(θ∣x) given in the table below:

    x1 x2 x3
    01 d e f
    02 a b c
    x1 x2 x3
    θ1: their heart condition 0.6912 0.0584 0.044
    θ2: their diabetes 0.3088 0.9416 0.956

    Observing this table we can see that if weight loss only is observed, the most plausible problem is their heart condition, if feeling dizzy only is observed, the most plausible problem is their diabetes, and if weight loss and feeling dizzy are observed, the most plausible problem is their diabetes. From these results, we can construct a diagnostic rule:

    Symptom Diagnosis
    weight loss only, (x1): their heart condition, θ1
    feeling dizzy only, (x2): their diabetes, θ2
    weight loss and feeling dizzy, (x3): their diabetes, θ2

    If we were to use this diagnostic rule repeatedly, then the probability of misdiagnosing the problem is:

    Pr(Misdiagnosis)=1−(Pr(θ1,X=x1)+Pr(θ2,X=x2)+Pr(θ2,X=x3))=1−(Pr(X=x1∣θ1)Pr(θ1)+Pr(X=x2∣θ2)Pr(θ2)+Pr(X=x3∣θ2)Pr(θ2))=1−(0.94×0.4+0.43×0.6+0.29×0.6)=1−0.808=0.192

    And so about 19.2% of diagnoses will be misdiagnoses.
